Module 1

Understanding JTBD Fundamentals

To comprehend the core tenets, history, and unifying principles of Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) theory.

üéØ What is Jobs to Be Done?

JTBD is a framework that focuses on understanding why customers "hire" products or services to get specific jobs done in their lives. Instead of focusing on your company, product features, or demographics, JTBD centers on the customer's objectives and progress they're trying to make.

üîë Core Principles

1. Focus on People's Objectives, Not Your Company

  • Traditional approach: "How can we sell more of our product?"
  • JTBD approach: "What is the customer trying to accomplish?"
üåü Example: Netflix

Netflix didn't just think about streaming video; they focused on the job "help me relax and be entertained at home." This led them to invest in original content, personalized recommendations, and binge-watching features.

2. Jobs are Stable Over Time

  • Technology changes, but human needs remain constant
  • People have always wanted to "communicate with distant friends" - the solution evolved from letters to phones to social media

3. People Seek Services That Make Jobs Quicker and Easier

  • Customers hire solutions that reduce time, effort, and friction
  • They fire solutions that create more work or complexity

4. Making Jobs the Unit of Analysis Makes Innovation Predictable

  • When you understand the job, you can predict what customers will value
  • You can innovate systematically rather than randomly

üöÄ Benefits of JTBD

  • Reduces innovation risk: Build what customers actually need
  • Common language: Aligns teams around customer value
  • Outside-in perspective: Shifts focus from internal capabilities to customer needs
  • Product-market fit: Ensures solutions match real jobs
üí° Workshop Activity

Reframe Your Product: Take your current main product/service. Write down:

  1. What job is your customer trying to get done? (Not what your product does, but what the customer wants to accomplish)
  2. How has this job been solved historically?
  3. What makes this job difficult today?
Module 2

Core Elements of JTBD

To identify and define the five core elements of the JTBD framework for precise analysis.

üéØ The Five Core Elements

1. Job Performer (WHO)

The individual executing the main job - the ultimate end-user (distinct from the buyer).

Other ecosystem roles: Approver, Reviewer, Technician, Manager, Audience, Assistant

üåü Example: Business Software
  • Job Performer: Sales rep entering customer data
  • Buyer: IT Director who purchases the software
  • Approver: CFO who approves the budget
  • Manager: Sales Manager who monitors usage

2. Jobs (WHAT)

The aim of the performer - what they want to accomplish, independent of any solution.

  • Main Job: Primary objective
  • Related Jobs: Connected activities
  • Emotional Jobs: How they want to feel
  • Social Jobs: How they want to be perceived
Job Statement Format: VERB + OBJECT + CLARIFIER
üåü Job Statement Examples
  • "Visit family on special occasions"
  • "Prepare healthy meals for weeknight dinners"
  • "Track project progress across multiple teams"
  • "Learn new skills to advance my career"

3. Process (HOW)

The procedure or sequence of stages the job performer goes through to accomplish the objective.

4. Needs (WHY)

The performer's requirements or intended outcomes during the job process.

Needs Statement Format: DIRECTION + MEASURE + OBJECT + CLARIFIER
üåü Needs Statement Examples
  • "Minimize the time it takes to find parking near the destination"
  • "Increase the accuracy of expense report calculations"
  • "Reduce the effort required to share files with team members"

5. Circumstances (WHEN/WHERE)

Contextual factors that frame job execution: time, manner, place, and situation.

üåü Circumstances Examples
  • Time: "During rush hour commute"
  • Place: "While traveling for business"
  • Situation: "When working with a tight deadline"
  • Manner: "While multitasking other responsibilities"

üìä Job Hierarchy

Jobs can be viewed at different levels of abstraction:

  • Aspirations: Highest level life goals
  • Big Jobs: Major objectives
  • Little Jobs: Specific tasks within big jobs
  • Micro-Jobs: Smallest actionable steps

Navigation: Ask "Why?" to move up, "How?" to move down

üåü Job Hierarchy Example
  • Aspiration: "Be a successful professional"
  • Big Job: "Stay informed about industry trends"
  • Little Job: "Read relevant articles during commute"
  • Micro-Job: "Save article to read later"
üí° Workshop Activity

Define the Five Elements: Select a common customer problem your team faces. Work together to define:

  1. Job Performer: Who is doing the main job?
  2. Job: What are they trying to accomplish? (Write as verb + object + clarifier)
  3. Process: What steps do they go through?
  4. Needs: What outcomes do they want? (Write 3-5 needs statements)
  5. Circumstances: When/where does this job happen?
Module 3

Discovering Value: Jobs & Switch Interviews

To learn and apply qualitative interviewing techniques to uncover customer goals and motivations.

üéØ Jobs Interviews

Purpose: Open-ended interviews with job performers to understand their objectives and needs, independent of products.

Key Focus Areas:

  • Background and context
  • Main and related jobs
  • Current process and workflow
  • Needs and desired outcomes
  • Circumstances and constraints

Critical Incident Technique

Ask about specific, recent experiences rather than general opinions:

  • Instead of: "How do you usually prepare for meetings?"
  • Ask: "Tell me about the last important meeting you prepared for. Walk me through exactly what you did."
üåü Jobs Interview Questions
  • "Walk me through the last time you had to [do the job]"
  • "What were you trying to accomplish?"
  • "What made this situation different or challenging?"
  • "What did you do before and after?"
  • "What would have made this easier?"
  • "How did you know you were successful?"

üîÑ Switch Interviews

Purpose: Reverse-engineer why people switch from one solution to another by recreating their purchase journey.

Timeline Stages:

  1. First Thought: What triggered consideration of change?
  2. Passively Looking: Casual exploration of alternatives
  3. Actively Looking: Serious comparison of options
  4. Deciding: Final selection process
  5. Consuming: First use experience
  6. Satisfaction: Ongoing value realization
üåü Switch Interview Example: Software Purchase

First Thought: "Our spreadsheet crashed and we lost hours of work"

Passively Looking: "I asked colleagues what they use"

Actively Looking: "I demo'd three different tools"

Deciding: "The free trial convinced me it was worth the cost"

Consuming: "Setup was easier than expected"

Satisfaction: "We haven't lost data since, and reports are faster"

Switch Interview Questions

  • "Tell me about the first time you thought about switching..."
  • "What happened that made you start looking?"
  • "How did you go about evaluating options?"
  • "What almost stopped you from switching?"
  • "What convinced you to make the final decision?"
  • "How did the first experience compare to expectations?"

üìã Interview Best Practices

  • Listen more than you talk: 80/20 rule
  • Ask "why" and "help me understand" to dig deeper
  • Focus on behavior, not opinions: What they did, not what they think
  • Stay solution-agnostic: Don't mention your product
  • Record for later analysis: You can't capture everything in notes
üí° Workshop Activity

Practice Interview: Pair up and conduct a 15-minute mock interview:

  1. Choose a common job (e.g., "plan a vacation," "find a new apartment")
  2. Interviewer: Use Jobs Interview techniques to understand the process
  3. Switch roles and try a Switch Interview about a recent purchase
  4. Debrief: What insights surprised you?
Module 4

Analysing Progress with Four Forces

To analyse interview insights using the Four Forces model to understand drivers and blockers of customer behaviour change.

‚öñÔ∏è The Four Forces Model

Four forces influence every customer's decision to switch from their current solution to a new one. A switch occurs when the forces for change (Push + Pull) outweigh the forces against change (Anxiety + Habit).

Force Direction Description Examples
Push Away from current Problems with current solution or external pressures System crashes, high costs, new regulations
Pull Toward new solution Attraction of new benefits or aspirations Better features, cost savings, status
Anxiety Against change Concerns about risks of switching Learning curve, integration issues, cost
Habit Against change Comfort with current routines and familiarity Muscle memory, established workflows

üéØ Detailed Force Descriptions

Push Forces

  • Functional problems: Current solution doesn't work properly
  • Economic pressure: Cost becomes unsustainable
  • External changes: New regulations, market shifts
  • Growth constraints: Current solution can't scale
  • Frustration: Accumulated annoyances reach tipping point

Pull Forces

  • Better performance: New solution works faster/better
  • Economic benefit: Cost savings or ROI improvement
  • Status/Image: New solution enhances reputation
  • New capabilities: Features that enable new possibilities
  • Aspirational: Helps achieve bigger goals

Anxiety Forces

  • Learning curve: Time and effort to master new solution
  • Integration risk: Will it work with existing systems?
  • Financial risk: What if it doesn't deliver promised value?
  • Social risk: What will others think of this choice?
  • Opportunity cost: Time spent switching vs. other priorities

Habit Forces

  • Muscle memory: Physical comfort with current tools
  • Mental models: Established ways of thinking
  • Workflow integration: Current solution fits existing process
  • Investment protection: Already paid for current solution
  • Inertia: "If it ain't broke, don't fix it"

üìä Using Four Forces Analysis

üåü Example: Switching to Project Management Software

Push: Email threads getting confusing, missing deadlines, hard to track progress

Pull: See organized dashboards, real-time updates, better team collaboration

Anxiety: Team might resist learning new tool, cost of subscription, integration with existing tools

Habit: Everyone knows how to use email, established meeting rhythms, current process "works"

Strategic Implications

  • Strengthen Push: Help customers recognize problems with status quo
  • Amplify Pull: Clearly communicate benefits and outcomes
  • Reduce Anxiety: Provide guarantees, trials, support, testimonials
  • Lower Habit barriers: Make switching easy, provide migration tools
üí° Workshop Activity

Four Forces Analysis: Take insights from a customer interview (real or hypothetical) and categorize them:

  1. Create four quadrants: Push, Pull, Anxiety, Habit
  2. Place customer quotes/insights in appropriate quadrants
  3. Identify which forces are strongest
  4. Brainstorm how your product could address each force
  5. Discuss: What would tip the balance toward switching?
Module 5

Mapping the Main Job

To create a chronological job map that visualizes the flow of the main job, identifying opportunities for innovation.

üó∫Ô∏è What is a Job Map?

A job map is a chronological representation of the main job's stages, showing the underlying patterns of what the customer is trying to accomplish - independent of any specific solution or brand.

Key Distinction: This is NOT a customer journey map (which shows interaction with your brand), but a job map (which shows the customer's process regardless of solutions used).

üìã Universal Job Map Structure

Most jobs follow a similar pattern with eight standard phases:

Phase Description Example Questions
Define Determine what needs to be done and success criteria What outcome do I want? What constraints exist?
Locate Find resources, information, or tools needed Where can I find what I need? Who can help?
Prepare Gather materials and set up for execution What do I need to get ready? How should I organize?
Confirm Verify readiness and validate approach Am I ready to proceed? Is this the right approach?
Execute Perform the core activity How do I complete the main task efficiently?
Monitor Track progress and ensure quality How am I doing? Is it working as expected?
Modify Make adjustments based on monitoring What needs to change? How can I improve?
Conclude Finish the job and evaluate success Did I achieve my goal? What did I learn?

üç≥ Example: "Prepare a Weeknight Dinner" Job Map

üåü Complete Job Map Example
  1. Define: Determine what kind of meal fits tonight's constraints (time, dietary needs, preferences)
  2. Locate: Find recipes and check what ingredients are available at home
  3. Prepare: Gather ingredients, tools, and prep workspace
  4. Confirm: Verify all ingredients are fresh and recipe is clear
  5. Execute: Cook the meal following the process
  6. Monitor: Check cooking progress, taste, and adjust seasoning
  7. Modify: Make adjustments to cooking time, temperature, or seasoning
  8. Conclude: Plate the meal, serve to family, clean up

‚úÖ Job Map Quality Guidelines

Good Job Map Stages Should Be:

  • Functional: Focus on what's being accomplished, not emotions
  • Short: 2-8 words per stage
  • Timeless: Would apply regardless of technology era
  • Solution-agnostic: No mention of specific tools or brands
  • Chronological: Follow natural sequence of activities

Avoid These Common Mistakes:

  • ‚ùå Emotional language: "Feel confident about choice"
  • ‚ùå Solution references: "Open spreadsheet application"
  • ‚ùå Adjectives: "Quickly gather materials"
  • ‚ùå Brand mentions: "Search on Google"
  • ‚úÖ Better: "Locate relevant information"

üí° Using Job Maps for Innovation

Job maps help identify innovation opportunities by revealing:

  • Pain points: Which stages are most difficult or time-consuming?
  • Unmet needs: What outcomes are customers seeking at each stage?
  • Workflow gaps: Where do customers struggle between stages?
  • Integration opportunities: How can stages be combined or streamlined?
üí° Workshop Activity

Create a Job Map: Choose a familiar main job and map it out:

  1. Pick a job relevant to your business (e.g., "hire a new employee," "plan a marketing campaign")
  2. Start with the universal 8-stage structure
  3. Customize each stage for your specific job
  4. Review against quality guidelines
  5. Identify the 2-3 stages that seem most problematic
  6. Brainstorm: How could technology or services make these stages easier?
Module 6

Defining Value: Underserved Needs

To identify and prioritize customer needs that are important but not well-satisfied by existing solutions.

üéØ What are Underserved Needs?

Underserved needs are customer outcomes that are:

  • Important to the customer (they care about this outcome)
  • Poorly satisfied by current solutions (there's a gap)

These represent the biggest opportunities for innovation and market success.

üìä Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)

ODI is a quantitative method developed by Tony Ulwick that measures underserved needs by surveying job performers on two dimensions for each desired outcome:

  • Importance: How important is this outcome? (1-5 scale)
  • Satisfaction: How well are current solutions achieving this? (1-5 scale)
Opportunity Score = Importance + (Importance - Satisfaction)

Interpreting Opportunity Scores

  • 15+: Extremely underserved (high opportunity)
  • 12-15: Very underserved (good opportunity)
  • 10-12: Underserved (some opportunity)
  • Below 10: Served or overserved (low opportunity)

üìù Writing Good Needs Statements

Needs statements should be specific, measurable, and solution-agnostic:

Direction + Metric + Object + Context
üåü Good Needs Statement Examples
  • "Minimize the time it takes to gather documents when preparing taxes"
  • "Increase the accuracy of expense calculations when submitting reports"
  • "Reduce the effort required to find relevant information during research"
  • "Maximize the likelihood of identifying errors before publication"

Avoid These Common Mistakes:

  • ‚ùå Solution-specific: "Make the software faster"
  • ‚ùå Vague: "Improve the user experience"
  • ‚ùå Emotional: "Feel more confident"
  • ‚úÖ Better: "Reduce the time it takes to complete data entry tasks"

üìà Alternative Prioritization Methods

1. Importance-Satisfaction Matrix

Plot job steps or needs on a 2x2 matrix:

  • High Importance, Low Satisfaction: Primary opportunities
  • High Importance, High Satisfaction: Table stakes (must maintain)
  • Low Importance, Low Satisfaction: Don't invest
  • Low Importance, High Satisfaction: Possible over-engineering

2. Jobs Scoring Sheet

Rate each job or need on three factors:

  • Importance: How critical is this to success?
  • Frequency: How often does this occur?
  • Frustration: How painful is the current solution?

Total score = (Importance √ó 3) + (Frequency √ó 2) + (Frustration √ó 2)

üéØ From Needs to Features

Once you've identified underserved needs, the next step is brainstorming solutions:

  1. Generate multiple concepts: Don't jump to the first idea
  2. Stay solution-agnostic initially: Explore different approaches
  3. Consider the whole ecosystem: Product, service, and process solutions
  4. Test assumptions: Validate that your solution actually addresses the need
üåü Example: Addressing "Minimize time to find parking"

Need: "Minimize the time it takes to find parking near the destination"

Possible Solutions:

  • Real-time parking availability app
  • Reserved parking booking system
  • Dynamic pricing to balance demand
  • Alternative transportation options
  • Better parking signage and wayfinding
üí° Workshop Activity

Identify Underserved Needs: For a chosen main job:

  1. Brainstorm 10-15 desired outcomes/needs customers have
  2. Write them as proper needs statements (direction + metric + object + context)
  3. As a team, rate each need on Importance (1-5) and current Satisfaction (1-5)
  4. Calculate opportunity scores or plot on importance-satisfaction matrix
  5. Identify the top 3 underserved needs
  6. Discuss: What solutions could address these needs?

Defining Value: Goal-Based Personas

To create personas based on customer goals and needs, rather than demographics, to inform design and innovation.

üë§ What are Goal-Based Personas?

Goal-based personas are archetypal representations of users that focus on:

  • Intended outcomes: What they're trying to achieve
  • Frustrations: What's blocking their progress
  • Success criteria: How they define success
  • Behavioral variables: Circumstances, attitudes, and background that affect their approach

Key Difference: Traditional personas focus on demographics ("who they are"), while goal-based personas focus on objectives ("what they're trying to accomplish").

üîç Behavioral Variables

These variables help differentiate how different people approach the same job:

Experience Level

  • Novice: Needs guidance, prefers simple options
  • Intermediate: Wants efficiency, some customization
  • Expert: Demands control, advanced features

Risk Tolerance

  • Risk-averse: Wants proven solutions, lots of validation
  • Risk-neutral: Balanced approach to new vs. established
  • Risk-seeking: Willing to try new approaches, early adopter

Time Availability

  • Time-constrained: Needs quick, efficient solutions
  • Time-flexible: Can invest time for better outcomes

Decision-Making Style

  • Analytical: Wants detailed information and comparisons
  • Intuitive: Relies on gut feel and recommendations
  • Consensus-driven: Needs input from others

üìù Goal-Based Persona Template

üåü Example: Project Management Software

The Overwhelmed Organizer

Main Job: "Coordinate team projects across multiple initiatives"

Key Goals:

  • Keep all team members aligned on priorities
  • Identify potential delays before they become critical
  • Reduce time spent in status update meetings

Current Frustrations:

  • Information scattered across email, chat, and documents
  • Difficult to get real-time project status
  • Team members forget to update progress

Success Looks Like:

  • Can see project status at a glance
  • Team proactively communicates issues
  • Fewer emergency "where are we?" meetings

Behavioral Variables:

  • Experience: Intermediate (managed projects before, not a PM expert)
  • Time: Highly constrained (juggling multiple responsibilities)
  • Decision style: Consensus-driven (needs team buy-in for tools)
  • Tech comfort: Moderate (can learn new tools but prefers intuitive)

‚ö° Proto-Personas

Proto-personas are quick, assumption-based personas created in workshops without formal research. They're useful for:

  • Getting teams aligned on target users
  • Identifying assumptions to test later
  • Starting design discussions
  • Planning research priorities

Important: Proto-personas should be validated with real customer research.

üéØ Using Personas in Development

Goal-based personas help teams by:

  • Prioritizing features: Which persona needs this most?
  • Design decisions: How would [persona] want to accomplish this?
  • User story writing: "As [persona], I want to..."
  • Testing scenarios: Role-play different persona approaches
  • Marketing messaging: What resonates with each persona?
üí° Workshop Activity

Create a Proto-Persona: Based on your team's knowledge of customers:

  1. Choose a key job your product addresses
  2. Identify 2-3 different types of people who do this job
  3. For each type, create a goal-based persona including:
    • Persona name (descriptive, not personal)
    • Main job they're trying to do
    • Top 3 goals within that job
    • Top 3 current frustrations
    • What success looks like to them
    • Key behavioral variables
  4. Discuss: How would your product need to be different for each persona?

Defining Value: Competitive Analysis

To compare competing solutions from a JTBD perspective, identifying unmet needs and strategic opportunities beyond traditional product categories.

üîÑ JTBD View of Competition

Traditional view: Companies compete with similar products in the same category.

JTBD view: Companies compete with any solution that helps customers get the same job done, regardless of product category.

This solution-agnostic perspective reveals unexpected competitors and opportunities.

üåü Example: "Get work done while commuting"

Traditional competitors: Other mobile productivity apps

JTBD competitors:

  • Audiobooks and podcasts (learning/development)
  • Voice recording apps (capturing ideas)
  • Phone calls (relationship building)
  • Music streaming (mental preparation)
  • Meditation apps (stress management)
  • Do nothing (rest and mental break)

üéØ Identifying Job-Based Competitors

Step 1: Define the Job Clearly

Be specific about the job you're analyzing. Different job definitions will reveal different competitive sets.

  • "Stay entertained during downtime" vs. "Learn new skills during commute"
  • "Track personal expenses" vs. "Prepare for tax season"
